# Hatch Brand Identity

Last updated: 2026-03-25 (America/Chicago)
Owner: Product + Design + Frontend Engineering

## Brand thesis
Hatch is where ideas are nurtured into real hackathons, teams, and projects.

The product should feel like the moment before launch: focused, warm, and full of contained energy. Hatch does not sell generic "innovation." It gives organizers the structure to turn an early concept into something people can join, build, judge, and remember.

## Brand promise
Give organizers the structure to launch events and give builders a place where ideas can take shape.

Hatch should communicate two truths at once:
- Organizers get a calm, reliable system for setup, submissions, judging, and publishing.
- Builders get a clear sense that this is where projects gather momentum and become real.

## Audience
Hatch speaks to both organizers and participants.

The homepage should feel welcoming to builders, clubs, and communities, while still keeping organizer conversion as the primary CTA path. Product language should not force the brand into an organizer-only enterprise voice or a builder-only community voice.

## Tone
Hatch should feel:
- Minimal
- Warm
- Confident
- Editorial
- Human

Hatch should not feel:
- Cold enterprise
- Playful mascot brand
- Overly cyber
- Buzzword-heavy

## Core metaphors
The visual and verbal system should draw from:
- Incubation
- Shell
- Crack
- Inner glow
- Emergence
- Readiness
- Momentum

These metaphors should stay abstract and restrained. The goal is to suggest a contained idea breaking open into something public and participatory.

## Visual principles
- High contrast over colorful noise
- Generous whitespace over crowded dashboards
- Restrained accent color over rainbow palettes
- Subtle symbolism over literal illustration
- Strong typography over decorative chrome

Preferred visual cues:
- shell curves
- fissure or crack lines
- low, warm inner glow
- structured layouts with breathing room

Avoid:
- cartoon eggs, chicks, or nests
- sticker-collage compositions
- heavy glassmorphism
- novelty motion that distracts from the message

## Copy principles
- Use short declarative headlines
- Keep product language grounded and operational
- Write with calm confidence, not hype
- Let the "hatching" metaphor support the message instead of replacing clarity

Avoid:
- vague claims about innovation
- fluffy startup language
- overly cute egg puns
- metaphor-heavy copy that hides what the product actually does

## Do / do not
Do:
- Use abstract crack, shell, and glow cues
- Keep organizer actions clear and direct
- Present Hatch as a place where events and ideas become real
- Balance warmth with structure

Do not:
- Expose `Warm Nest Lab` as public-facing product language
- Turn the brand into a mascot system
- Overload the homepage with decorative visuals
- Let minimalism become cold or empty

## Homepage application
### Hero
Lead with a short statement about hatching ideas into real hackathons or launches. Pair it with concise product copy and two clear actions: one to start hosting and one to explore events.

### Proof band
Use a sparse proof strip to show that Hatch is practical: fast to launch, judging-ready, public-facing, and built for event operations.

### Feature narrative
Frame the core product around what Hatch helps people do:
- shape the event
- bring people in
- review what emerges
- publish what hatched

### Events preview
Treat live events as evidence of the brand promise. The section should show that Hatch is not theoretical; it is where ideas become visible, structured, and joinable.

### Closing CTA
End on calm conviction. The page should leave people with the feeling that Hatch is the simplest serious way to turn an event idea into something real.
